# Wireless Broadcast Gossip for Decentralized Drone Swarms: Success Probability, Contraction, and Optimal Aloha 

Ali Khalesi 

Abstract —We study broadcast gossip for decentralized drone swarms over an interference-limited wireless medium. Modeling drone locations as a planar Poisson point process and medium access via slotted Aloha, we derive (i) a closed-form SIR success probability under Rayleigh fading, (ii) a mean-square contraction bound in which the consensus rate factorizes into an ideal mixing term and an explicit wireless thinning term, and (iii) a closed-form access probability that optimizes a sharp availability– reliability proxy. Simulations corroborate the predicted operating point by matching the fastest convergence region. 

Index Terms —Gossip, consensus, UAV swarms, stochastic ge-ometry, Aloha, SIR. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Drone swarms increasingly perform coordination tasks without a central controller: keeping a formation, aligning headings, agreeing on a shared reference frame, and fusing local measurements to build a common estimate. In many practical settings, the only reliable resource is short-range 

broadcast communication: each drone periodically sends a small message that nearby drones can hear. Because these updates are local and do not require routing or a leader, 

consensus methods based on simple pairwise averaging (often called gossip algorithms 1) are a natural fit for swarms [2], [9], [10]. A key difficulty is that swarm communication does not occur over reliable links. Broadcasts share the same wireless channel, so simultaneous transmissions interfere with each other. As a result, whether a packet is decoded is random and depends on how many other drones transmit at the same time and how close they are. This means that the speed of a gossip loop is determined not only by who could talk to whom 

(the neighborhood graph), but also by how often messages are successfully received under interference. Capturing this interaction between the wireless layer and the algorithm is the main goal of this letter. While we use a standard SIR-based decoding model to capture broadcast reliability, the focus is not on physical-layer design. Rather, we translate the resulting reliability law into an explicit MAC-level tuning rule that accelerates network-layer gossip. A standard way to obtain tractable yet realistic reliability laws is stochastic geometry 2. In particular, modeling node locations by a Poisson point process (PPP) 3 leads to closed-form expressions for the probability that a wireless transmission succeeds under interference. These tools were developed extensively in the Poisson-network literature, in-cluding influential work by Baccelli and collaborators; see, e.g., the monographs [4], [5] and studies of random access and Aloha-type protocols in Poisson networks [6]–[8]. A recurring outcome of this line of work is that, with Rayleigh fading 4

and power-law path loss, the probability of meeting a target 

signal-to-interference ratio (SIR) 5 has a simple exponential form, which enables explicit protocol design. We adopt a snapshot model appropriate for fast swarm-control loops. Drone locations are modeled as a planar PPP of intensity λ, and time is slotted. In each slot, each drone trans-mits with probability p using slotted Aloha 6. Slots are assumed short relative to mobility, so the geometry is effectively con-stant over the convergence horizon of interest. Each listening drone considers neighbors within a fixed range R and attempts to decode one nearby broadcast. Because successful broadcast reception is inherently one-way, we introduce a lightweight 

matching abstraction: the set of successful exchanges in a slot is implemented as a matching (e.g., via simple backoff and acknowledgments), so that each executed exchange becomes a symmetric pairwise averaging step. This ensures that the update preserves the global average and can be analyzed with standard disagreement measures (Section II).

C ONCLUSION 

This letter studied a simple but practically relevant question for decentralized drone swarms: how fast can broadcast gossip mix information when the wireless medium is interference-limited and access is random. Using a Poisson spatial model with slotted Aloha and SIR-based decoding, we obtained three complementary results: (i) a closed-form reception probability that makes the dependence on density, access probability, distance, and the SIR threshold explicit (Theorem 1); (ii) a mean-square contraction bound showing that the consensus rate factorizes into an algorithmic term (how strongly an averaging exchange contracts disagreement) and a wireless 

term (how often such exchanges occur under interference) (Theorem 2); and (iii) an explicit operating point p⋆ that optimizes a sharp availability–reliability proxy and yields the interpretable scaling p⋆ = Θ(1 /λ ) for fixed (R, θ, α )

(Proposition 1). The simulation in Section IV illustrates the resulting design message: the convergence curve is unimodal in p, and the predicted p⋆ falls in the fastest-mixing region, turning the analysis into a concrete MAC-level tuning rule for dense swarms. 

a) Future work.: A natural next step is to move beyond the static snapshot and quantify how mobility reshapes both interference and mixing. In particular, it is worth investigating a model where each drone moves in a uniformly random direction with heterogeneous speeds, and studying how the resulting time-varying geometry changes the effective success probability, the matching opportunities, and the contraction behavior. Understanding when mobility accelerates agreement (by creating time diversity and new neighbors) versus when it slows it down (through increased link variability) would further strengthen wireless broadcast gossip as a lightweight coordination primitive for large-scale swarms.
