# Revisiting the expressiveness of metric temporal logic: A tale of “Je t’aime, moi non plus.” 

## Mohammed Aristide Foughali 

IRIF, Université Paris Cité 

Abstract 

The expressiveness of Metric Temporal Logic (MTL) has been extensively studied throughout the last two decades. In particular, it has been shown that the interval-based semantics of MTL is strictly more expressive than the pointwise one. These results may suggest that enabling the evaluation of formulae at arbitrary time points instead of positions of timed events increases the expressive power of MTL. In this paper, we formally argue otherwise. We demonstrate that under standard models of finite or non-Zeno infinite (action-based) timed executions, the interval-based and the pointwise semantics are incomparable, and therefore disprove a twenty-year-old result. We then propose a new mixed semantics that embeds both the pointwise and the interval-based ones.

## 1 Introduction 

Metric Temporal Logic (MTL) [ 20 ] is a popular timed extension of the Linear-time Temporal Logic LTL [ 26 ]. The importance of MTL motivated a large body of research investigating its expressive power [ 13 , 8 , 9, 18 , 25 , 17 ]. In this paper, we focus on the expressiveness of the 

pointwise semantics of MTL as compared to its interval-based one. In order to present the existing results on such comparison, we first (informally) introduce the model of timed words .A timed event is a couple containing a letter, representing an action , and a real number corresponding to its timestamp , i.e., the global time at which it occurred. A timed word is a finite or infinite sequence of timed events. Following the least constrained model of timed words [ 1, 23 , 24 ], which we refer to hereafter as the general model , a (finite or infinite) timed word satisfies time monotonicity, i.e., timestamps are non-decreasing, and an infinite timed word obeys time divergence (also called non-Zenoness), that is global time does not converge towards some bounded value. For instance, over the alphabet {a, b, c }, the sequence (a, 0)( b, 1)( c, 1)( a, 5.3) is a finite timed word whereas (a, 0)( b, 1)( c, 0.8) is not (time monotonicity is violated). Similarly, the infinite timed word (a, 12 )( b, 34 )( b, 78 )( c, 15 16 ) . . . , i.e., where the difference between two consecutive timestamps is halved at every next action, is excluded (global time converges towards 1). Under the pointwise semantics, formulae are evaluated at the positions of timed events within the timed word, whereas the interval-based semantics is given over continuous intervals within, typically, a timed signal [25 ]. To enable its comparison with the pointwise semantics, two different approaches have been adopted w.r.t the interval-based semantics. In the first one, D’Souza and Prabhakar [ 13 ] rely on timed words, allowing the evaluation of formulae at arbitrary time points (including those where no events occur, i.e., not corresponding to a timestamp). The second approach, used in Bouyer et al. [ 8 , 9], operates over a timed state sequence , representing indirectly a timed word. Both works show that the interval-based semantics is more expressive than the pointwise one as we elaborate next. On the one hand, D’Souza and Prabhakar [ 13 ] demonstrate such important result over finite timed words, using a constrained model where monotonicity is strict ( e.g., the timed word (a, 0)( b, 1)( c, 1)( a, 5.3) used as an example above is excluded by design). We argue that, while this choice is reasonable is many cases, it is not justified in general. Indeed, simultaneous

Despite their importance, ER1 and ER2 were obtained under different assumptions; they provide thus a partial answer regarding the comparability of the two semantics. There is therefore a need to complete the picture and, in case of incomparability, unify these semantics in order to benefit from their respective advantages. 

Contributions. In this paper, we (i) answer the comparability question by the negative under the general model of timed words and (ii) devise a new mixed semantics that unifies the pointwise and the interval-based ones. More precisely, we provide the following contributions: 

C1 The interval-based and the pointwise semantics are incomparable under the general model of finite timed words, 

C2 The interval-based and the pointwise semantics are incomparable under the general model of infinite timed words, 

C3 The incomparability holds even under reasonable restrictions such as non-stuttering ,

C4 A mixed semantics embedding the interval-based and the pointwise ones is proposed. 

C1 , C2 and C3 complete the picture of ER1 and ER2 , showing that the former holds no more under the more general (non-strict) time monotonicity whereas the latter is unfortunately inaccurate. In addition, C4 reconciles the interval-based and the pointwise semantics, conjugating their full expressive powers.

## 2.3 Timed words vs. timed state sequences 

A timed word is a natural representation of an action-based execution of a timed system. For example, (a, 2)( b, 2)( c, 4.1) corresponds to an execution where the system waited for two time units, performed action a then immediately b, then waited for 2.1 time units and finally performed c. A timed state sequence, on the other hand, is suitable for signals: e.g., M.A. Foughali 5

While there is a two-way translation between the untimed versions of these models modulo bisimulation [ 11 ], this is not the case in the timed setting, see e.g., the discussion on signals vs. timed words in [ 6] (this heterogeneity led to the proposition of weakly-monotonic timed state sequences [ 2 ] and signal-event frameworks as in [ 7]). We adopt an action-based view for the simple reason that it is the only way to enable any comparison involving the pointwise semantics. We still need timed state sequences as they were cleverly used in Bouyer et al. [ 8, 9] whose side expressiveness result (Section 1,

## 3.2 Approach 3.2.1 Equating |=its and |=itw 

In order to prove the incomparability between the pointwise and interval-based semantics, we first need to deal with the heterogeneity between timed words and timed state sequences. In other words, there is no way to compare |=pw and |=its if we allow arbitrary timed state sequences, due to the notable difference in nature with timed words, as explained in Section 2.3. For example, the timed state sequence ({a, b }, [0 , 1))( {c}, [1 , 1]) does not make much sense in the context of action-based timed executions: actions a and b, atomic by definition, cannot remain true continuously over the dense interval [0 , 1) .We need therefore to isolate the largest subset of timed state sequences that faithfully represents action-based timed executions modulo the interval-based semantics. We show that such subset, which we call Ka, can be obtained using natural properties of action-based timed executions. In mathematical terms, Ka is the largest set of images of elements of P by a total relation R guaranteeing that |=its and |=itw coincide. 

Action-based timed state sequences. To construct Ka, we use the fact that actions occur only at punctual intervals. Then, since the first action occurs at 0 and the execution, if finite, terminates with an action, the first interval is [0 , 0] and the last interval, if exists, is punctual. Finally, between every two punctual intervals, we have a non-punctual one with no action associated. This gives us an alternation of punctual intervals with sets of actions at even positions and non-punctual ones with empty sets at odd positions.

## 4 Mixed Semantics 

In this section, we present a new mixed semantics that embeds both the pointwise and the interval-based ones, therefore unifying their expressive power. We do so in three steps. First, we present an intuitive version of the mixed semantics over MTL that leverages the expressive power of the interval-based semantics to increase that of the pointwise one (Section 4.1). Second, in Section 4.2, we conjecture that such version, while strictly more expressive than the poitwise semantics, is incomparable with the interval-based one (due mainly to the fact that the latter is more suited for signals). Third, we show that extending the syntax of MTL with a single atomic formula unleashes the power of the mixed semantics leading to embedding both the pointwise and the interval-based ones (Section 4.3). In the next subsection, we show that this need of “immediate past” is an overkill as we can instead use a single atomic formulae to extend the syntax of MTL in order to embed the full expressive power of |=itw into |=mx .

In particular, we emphasise the simplicity of the extension of MTL (through the single atomic formula β) to sum the powers of both semantics, as well as the automated compilers L−Mpw and L−Mitw to move from the extended mixed semantics to the pointwise and interval-based ones while preserving the languages defined by formulae. 

## 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we revisited an important aspect of the extensively studied expressiveness of MTL, namely that of its pointwise semantics as opposed to the interval-based one. We have demonstrated that the two semantics are incomparable over the general models of action-based timed executions, including timed words and action-based timed state sequences. Our results disprove a twenty-year-old finding and gives a clear answer, by the negative, to the question on whether reasoning over arbitrary time points instead of ordered positions of timed events increases the expressive power of MTL. Another takeaway is the fact that the pointwise semantics is more suitable for action-based timed executions whereas the interval-based one is more adapted to signals. To get the better of both worlds over action-based executions, we proposed a new mixed semantics that embeds both the interval-based and the pointwise ones. For future work, it would be interesting to consider mixed sequences where our mixed semantics could be further extended to reason on mixed signal-event executions while maintaining reasonable complexity. This would require to define a mixed structure, e.g., 

(( a, b ), [0 , 0])( {p, q }, (0 , 1))(( c), [1 , 1]) where a, b and c are in the actions set and p,q in the atomic propositions one, then further extend our mixed semantics to both sets. Another line of future work would be to relate the mixed semantics to the different semantics of more expressive logics such as TPTL [3] and T lin [5].
